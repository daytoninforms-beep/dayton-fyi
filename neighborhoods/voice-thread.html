Jared is right that we need honesty. But I'd push back on one thing — the conversation can't just be about guns. In Five Oaks, the violence is inseparable from vacant properties, no jobs for young people, and a rec center that closes at 5pm. We need the commission to see these as one problem, not three budget lines.

The ALPR camera vote in January was a perfect example — four commissioners voted yes on surveillance technology without ever discussing whether the $180K could have gone to the Northwest Rec Center staying open until 9pm. That's not a conversation about gun violence. That's a conversation about what kind of safety we're actually building.

As a Documenter who covered the ALPR camera vote in January, I can add context: Commissioner Joseph was the only vote against, citing privacy concerns for immigrant communities in northeast Dayton. That vote is directly relevant to the surveillance vs. safety tradeoff Jared describes.

The meeting minutes don't capture the full picture — during citizen comment, three Old North Dayton residents spoke about feeling watched rather than protected. The Documenter notes from that meeting are in the Commission Voting Record.
